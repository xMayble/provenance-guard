# Provenance Guard - Planning

Writing this before I build anything so I actually know what I'm making.

The idea: a backend a writing/art platform could use to check if a piece of text was
written by a person or generated by AI. It shouldn't just say yes or no though. It should
give a confidence level, show readers a clear label, keep a log of every decision, and let
creators appeal if they think it got them wrong.

One thing I want to keep in mind the whole way through: getting it wrong by calling a real
person's writing "AI" is worse than missing some AI text. On a writing site, falsely
accusing an actual author is the bad outcome. So I'm setting my thresholds and label
wording to be careful about that.

## The two detection signals

I need at least two signals that look at genuinely different things. Mine are:

### Signal 1: ask an LLM (Groq)

I send the text to Groq (llama-3.3-70b-versatile) and ask it how likely the text is
AI-written, and to give me back a number plus a quick reason.

- Output: a score from 0 to 1, where 1 means "definitely AI".
- Why it works: LLMs are good at spotting the kind of even, hedgy, cliche writing they
  tend to produce themselves ("It is important to note...", "Furthermore...").
- Where it fails: these detectors aren't actually reliable. They tend to flag formal or
  non-native English writing as AI even when it isn't, and the score can wobble a little
  each time you ask. So I treat it as one strong opinion, not the truth.

### Signal 2: stylometry (just math on the text, no libraries)

This looks at the shape of the writing instead of the meaning. I calculate a few things,
but only two of them actually feed the score. The other two I just show for context
because I don't trust them enough to score with.

The two I score on:

1. Burstiness = how much sentence length jumps around (standard deviation of words per
   sentence). People mix long and short sentences. AI tends to keep them all about the
   same length. Low variation looks more AI.
2. Informality = casual markers per 100 words: contractions, lowercase sentence starts,
   "...", "!!", ALL CAPS emphasis (3+ letters, so acronyms like "AI" don't count). Real
   casual writing has lots of these. AI text is usually clean. Few markers looks more AI.

The two I only report (don't score):

3. Type-token ratio (unique words / total words) - vocabulary variety.
4. Average sentence length.

I'm not scoring on those two because they track how formal/long the writing is, not
whether a human wrote it. A wordy human and a wordy AI both score high, so using them
would just make me look more confident than I should be.

The stylometry score combines the two I do trust, leaning on informality because it
separates casual human writing from clean AI text more reliably than burstiness does on
short samples:
`style_score = 0.35 * burstiness + 0.65 * informality`

### Putting the two signals together

`p_ai = 0.65 * llm_score + 0.35 * style_score`

I lean on the LLM more (0.65) because it reads the whole thing and is the stronger
detector. Stylometry gets 0.35 - it's a good sanity check because it's consistent and
doesn't change between runs, but it's pretty rough, so it backs up the LLM instead of
overruling it. That final `p_ai` number is my confidence score.

## What the confidence score means

`confidence` = `p_ai` = my estimate of the probability the text is AI, from 0 to 1.
0.5 means I genuinely can't tell. The further from 0.5, the more sure I am.

So a 0.6 sits in my "uncertain" zone - leaning AI but not enough to say so. The label
just says that honestly instead of rounding it up to an accusation.

My three zones (on purpose, the AI cutoff is high and the middle is wide, so I don't
accuse people unless I'm pretty sure):

| p_ai            | result        | label                 |
|-----------------|---------------|-----------------------|
| 0.70 and up     | likely_ai     | high-confidence AI    |
| 0.45 to 0.70    | uncertain     | uncertain             |
| under 0.45      | likely_human  | high-confidence human |

So 0.51 gives an uncertain label and 0.95 gives a strong AI label. There's no hard
flip at 0.5.

## The three labels (exact text)

`{pct}` is a percentage from the score.

High-confidence AI (p_ai >= 0.70, pct = round(p_ai*100)):
> 🤖 Likely AI-generated. Our automated analysis suggests this content was probably
> created with significant AI assistance (estimated {pct}% likelihood). This is an
> automated estimate, not a verdict, and detection isn't perfect. If you're the creator
> and think this is wrong, you can appeal and a human will review it.

High-confidence human (p_ai < 0.45, pct = round((1-p_ai)*100)):
> ✍️ Likely human-written. Our automated analysis didn't find strong signs of AI
> generation (estimated {pct}% likelihood this is human-written). This is a probability,
> shown just for context.

Uncertain (0.45 <= p_ai < 0.70, pct = round(p_ai*100)):
> ❓ Uncertain. We couldn't confidently tell whether this is human- or AI-written
> (estimated {pct}% AI-likelihood). Rather than guess, we're marking it inconclusive.

## Appeals

- Who: the creator of a piece of content, using its content_id.
- What they send: the content_id and creator_reasoning (why they think it's wrong).
- What happens:
  1. Look up the content by id (error if it doesn't exist).
  2. Change its status from "classified" to "under_review".
  3. Add an appeal entry to the audit log, kept next to the original decision (the
     attribution, confidence, and both signal scores) along with their reasoning and a time.
  4. Send back a confirmation.
- I'm not auto re-running detection on appeal (the spec says I don't have to). It goes to
  a human.
- A reviewer checking GET /log sees each under_review item with its original scores and
  the creator's reasoning right there, which is enough to make a call.

## Where I expect it to do badly

1. Simple, repetitive poems. Refrains and short even lines mean low burstiness and few
   casual markers, so stylometry will think it's AI even though that's just how the poem
   is written. The high AI cutoff, the LLM, and the appeal path are my safety nets here.
2. Formal or non-native English writing. Polished academic-style text is even and has no
   contractions, so both signals drift toward AI. This is exactly why my AI cutoff is high
   and why appeals exist.
3. Really short text (under ~40 words). Burstiness needs a few sentences to mean anything.
   On a one-liner it's basically noise, so I'd rather land in uncertain.
4. AI text a human lightly edited. That should land in the middle (uncertain), which is
   honestly the right answer.

## Architecture

### Submit flow

```
client
  |  POST /submit { text, creator_id }
  v
app.py (Flask)
  |- rate limiter checks the caller
  |- text --> Signal 1: llm_signal()   --> llm_score (0-1)
  |- text --> Signal 2: stylometry()   --> style_score (0-1)
  |- (llm_score, style_score) --> combine() --> p_ai (confidence)
  |- p_ai --> classify()   --> attribution
  |- p_ai --> make_label() --> label text
  |- everything --> store.record_submission() --> audit log
  v
JSON back: { content_id, attribution, confidence, label, signals, status, timestamp }
```

### Appeal flow

```
creator
  |  POST /appeal { content_id, creator_reasoning }
  v
app.py
  |- store.get_content(content_id)   (error if unknown)
  |- status: classified --> under_review
  |- store.record_appeal()  --> audit log (appeal + original decision + reasoning)
  v
JSON back: { content_id, status: "under_review", message }

GET /log --> store.read_log() --> { entries: [...] }   (newest first)
```

On submit, the text goes to both signals, their scores get combined into one confidence
number, that number decides the attribution and the label, and the whole thing is logged
before the response goes out. On appeal, the content_id finds the original record, flips
its status to under_review, and saves the creator's reasoning next to the original
decision so a human can look at it through GET /log.

## How I'm using an AI tool

I'm using an AI coding assistant to help write the code, but I'm giving it this spec
section by section so it builds what I actually designed, and I'm checking the output each
step instead of pasting blindly.

- M3 (submit endpoint + first signal): give it the Signal 1 section and the diagram. Ask
  for the Flask skeleton with POST /submit, the llm_signal() function, the JSON log
  helpers, and GET /log. Check: run llm_signal() on a couple of texts and look at the raw
  score before wiring it in, then curl /submit and check the JSON and the log entry.
- M4 (second signal + scoring): give it Signal 2, the combining part, and the confidence
  section. Ask for stylometry() and the combine()/classify() logic using my exact weights
  (0.65/0.35) and cutoffs (0.70 / 0.45). Check: run the four test texts and make sure the
  scores spread out into the right zones.
- M5 (production layer): give it the labels section and the appeals section. Ask for
  make_label() that returns my three exact label strings, the POST /appeal endpoint, and
  rate limiting on /submit. Check: hit all three label zones, appeal a real content_id and
  confirm GET /log shows under_review and the reasoning, and flood /submit to see the 429s.
